What's MVC
and why should I care?

Are you a developer? Do you like making your life easier? Then you should care a lot!

  • Next

MVC is an architectural design pattern for software. It stands for Model, View, Controller.

MVC isn’t a framework in and of itself, but many popular development frameworks use the MVC structure. We’re going to break down MVC using a popular Fruit themed tech store as an analogy. In our analogy, we’ll be looking at the front of the store, the mysterious back room, and the Fruit-branded Really Smart Person as different components of MVC. We’ll also be seeing the components out of acronym order, because customers don’t walk into the back of stores!

View

  • Renders what the user sees
  • Can be passed dynamic views from the controller
  • Is usually comprised of HTML, CSS, and JavaScript

The view is what the user sees. In this case, it's the meticulously curated front of our Fruit store. It's an experience made to present products (data) to our customer in a polished and easily navigable format. Our customers don't want to see a stack of products with barcodes and model numbers, so we obscure certain parts of our MVC applications and use controllers to deliver only the information we want users to see in the format we want them to see it.

Controller

  • Processes requests (GET, POST, PUT, DELETE)
  • Receives input
  • Gets data from model
  • Passes data to the view
  • Middlemans model and view

The Controller is our Really Smart Person. It takes requests from our user, interprets those requests against a router, sends them to the back room, grabs the requested product, puts it in a nice bag, and delivers it to the user.

Model

  • Defines the data an app should contain
  • Grabs data from the database
  • Passes the controller a data representation of what the user requested

The model deals with getting and manipulating data, logic, and rules of an application. This is the mysterious back room of our Fruit store: the user never sees what goes on back there (and doesn’t need to), but it’s essential for getting what they’re asking for. The back room houses all the products and prepares them for delivery to the customer.
